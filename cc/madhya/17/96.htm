Madhya-līlāChapter 17: The Lord Travels to Vrindāvana

Bhaktivedanta VedaBase: Śrī Caitanya Caritāmrita Madhya 17.96

shad-darśana-vyākhyā vinā kathā nāhi ethā

miśra kripā kari' more śunāna krishna-kathā

SYNONYMS

shat-darśana — of the six philosophical theses; vyākhyā — explanation; vinā — except; kathā — talk; nāhi — not; ethā — here; miśra — Tapana Miśra; kripā kari' — being very merciful; more — unto me; śunāna — explains; krishna-kathā — topics of Lord Śrī Krishna.

TRANSLATION

Candraśekhara continued, "There is no talk at Vārānasī other than discussions on the six philosophical theses. Nonetheless, Tapana Miśra has been very kind to me, for he speaks about topics relating to Lord Krishna.

PURPORT

The six philosophical theses are (1) Vaiśeshika, propounded by Kanāda Rishi, (2) Nyāya, propounded by Gautama Rishi, (3) Yoga, or mysticism, propounded by Patañjali Rishi, (4) the philosophy of Sāńkhya, propounded by Kapila Rishi, (5) the philosophy of Karma-mīmāmsā, propounded by Jaimini Rishi, and (6) the philosophy of Brahma-mīmāmsā, or Vedānta, the ultimate conclusion of the Absolute Truth (janmādy asya yatah [SB 1.1.1]), propounded by Vedavyāsa. Actually Vedānta philosophy is meant for the devotees because in the Bhagavad-gītā (15.15) Lord Krishna says, vedānta-krid veda-vid eva cāham: "I am the compiler of Vedānta, and I am the knower of the Vedas." Vyāsadeva is an incarnation of Krishna, and consequently Krishna is the compiler of Vedānta philosophy. Therefore Krishna clearly knows the purport of Vedānta philosophy. As stated in the Bhagavad-gītā, whoever hears Vedānta philosophy from Krishna is actually aware of the real meaning of Vedānta. The Māyāvādīs call themselves Vedāntists but do not at all understand the purport of Vedānta philosophy. Not being properly educated, people in general think that Vedānta means the Śańkarite interpretation.
