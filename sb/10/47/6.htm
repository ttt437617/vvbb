Canto 10: The Summum BonumChapter 47: The Song of the Bee

Bhaktivedanta VedaBase: Śrīmad Bhāgavatam 10.47.6

anyeshv artha-kritā maitrī

yāvad-artha-vidambanam

pumbhih strīshu kritā yadvat

sumanahsv iva shatpadaih

SYNONYMS

anyeshu — toward others; artha — for some motivation; kritā — manifested; maitrī — friendship; yāvat — for as long; artha — (as one is fulfilling his) motive; vidambanam — pretense; pumbhih — by men; strīshu — for women; kritā — shown; yadvat — as much; sumanahsu — for flowers; iva — as; shat-padaih — by bees.

TRANSLATION

The friendship shown toward others — those who are not family members — is motivated by personal interest, and thus it is a pretense that lasts only until one's purpose is fulfilled. Such friendship is just like the interest men take in women, or bees in flowers.

PURPORT

Śrīla Viśvanātha Cakravartī Thākura explains here that attractive women, like flowers, possess beauty, fragrance, tenderness, charm and so on. And as bees drink only once of a flower's nectar and then leave it for another, fickle men abandon beautiful and devoted women to pursue other pleasures. This tendency is condemned here by the gopīs, who gave their hearts completely to Śrī Krishna. The gopīs wanted only to exhibit their charms for Lord Krishna's pleasure, and in the pain of separation they questioned the motives of His friendship with them.

These are the transcendental pastimes of the Lord. Both Lord Krishna and the gopīs are completely liberated souls engaged in spiritual loving affairs. By contrast, our so-called loving affairs, being perverted reflections of the perfect loving relationships in the spiritual world, are polluted with lust, greed, pride and so on. Like all liberated souls, the gopīs — and certainly Lord Krishna Himself — are eternally free of these lower qualities, and their intense loving affairs are motivated exclusively by unalloyed devotion.
