Canto 10: The Summum BonumChapter 18: Lord Balarāma Slays the Demon Pralamba

Bhaktivedanta VedaBase: Śrīmad Bhāgavatam 10.18.12

bhramanair lańghanaih kshepair

āsphotana-vikarshanaih

cikrīdatur niyuddhena

kāka-paksha-dharau kvacit

SYNONYMS

bhramanaih — with whirling about; lańghanaih — jumping; kshepaih — throwing; āsphotana — slapping; vikarshanaih — and dragging; cikriīdatuh — They(Krishna and Balarāma) played; niyuddhena — with fighting; kāka-paksha — the locks of hair on the sides of Their heads; dharau — holding; kvacit — sometimes.

TRANSLATION

Krishna and Balarāma played with their cowherd boyfriends by whirling about, leaping, hurling, slapping and fighting. Sometimes Krishna and Balarāma would pull the hair on the boys' heads.

PURPORT

The ācāryas have explained this verse as follows: The word bhramanaih indicates that the boys, pretending they were machines, would sometimes

whirl about until they became dizzy. They would also sometimes jump about (lańghanaih). The word kshepaih indicates that sometimes they would hurl objects like balls or stones and that sometimes they would grab each other by the arms and throw one another about. Āsphotana means that sometimes they would slap one another's shoulders or backs, and vikarshanaih indicates they would drag one another about in the midst of their play. By the word niyuddhena arm wrestling and other types of friendly fighting are indicated, and the word kāka-paksha-dharau means that Krishna and Balarāma would sometimes grab the hair on the other boys' heads in a playful manner.
