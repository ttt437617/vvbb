Canto 4: Creation of the Fourth OrderChapter 26: King Purañjana Goes to the Forest to Hunt, and His Queen Becomes Angry

Bhaktivedanta VedaBase: Śrīmad Bhāgavatam 4.26.24

tasmin dadhe damam aham tava vīra-patni

yo 'nyatra bhūsura-kulāt krita-kilbishas tam

paśye na vīta-bhayam unmuditam tri-lokyām

anyatra vai mura-ripor itaratra dāsāt

SYNONYMS

tasmin — unto him; dadhe — shall give; damam — punishment; aham — I; tava — to you; vīra-patni — O wife of the hero; yah — one who; anyatra — besides; bhū-sura-kulāt — from the group of demigods on this earth (the brāhmanas); krita — done; kilbishah — offense; tam — him; paśye — I see; na — not; vīta — without; bhayam — fear; unmuditam — without anxiety; tri-lokyām — within the three worlds; anyatra — elsewhere; vai — certainly; mura-ripoh — of the enemy of Mura (Krishna); itaratra — on the other hand; dāsāt — than the servant.

TRANSLATION

O hero's wife, kindly tell me if someone has offended you. I am prepared to give such a person punishment as long as he does not belong to the brāhmana caste. But for the servant of Muraripu [Krishna], I excuse no one within or beyond these three worlds. No one can freely move after offending you, for I am prepared to punish him.

PURPORT

According to Vedic civilization, a brāhmana, or one who is properly qualified to understand the Absolute Truth — that is, one belonging to the most intelligent social order — as well as the devotee of Lord Krishna, who is known as Muradvisha, enemy of a demon named Mura, is not subject to the rules and regulations of the state. In other words, upon breaking the laws of the state, everyone can be punished by the government except the brāhmanas and Vaishnavas. Brāhmanas and Vaishnavas never transgress the laws of the state or the laws of nature because they know perfectly well the resultant reactions caused by such law-breaking. Even though they may sometimes appear to violate the laws, they are not to be punished by the king. This instruction was given to King Prācīnabarhishat by Nārada Muni. King Purañjana was a representative of King Prācīnabarhishat, and Nārada Muni was reminding King Prācīnabarhishat of his forefather, Mahārāja Prithu, who never chastised a brāhmana or a Vaishnava.

One's pure intelligence, or pure Krishna consciousness, becomes polluted by material activities. Pure consciousness can be revived by the process of sacrifice, charity, pious activities, etc., but when one pollutes his Krishna consciousness by offending a brāhmana or a Vaishnava, it is very difficult to revive. Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu has described the vaishnava-aparādha, or offense to a Vaishnava, as "the mad elephant offense." One should be very careful not to offend a Vaishnava or a brāhmana. Even the great yogī Durvāsā was harassed by the Sudarśana cakra when he offended the Vaishnava Mahārāja Ambarīsha, who was neither a brāhmana nor a sannyāsī but an ordinary householder. Mahārāja Ambarīsha was a Vaishnava, and consequently Durvāsā Muni was chastised.

The conclusion is that if Krishna consciousness is covered by material sins, one can eliminate the sins simply by chanting the Hare Krishna mantra, but if one pollutes his Krishna consciousness by offending a brāhmana or a Vaishnava, one cannot revive it until one properly atones for the sin by pleasing the offended Vaishnava or brāhmana. This was the course that Durvāsā Muni had to follow, for he surrendered unto Mahārāja Ambarīsha. A vaishnava-aparādha cannot be atoned for by any means other than by begging the pardon of the offended Vaishnava.
