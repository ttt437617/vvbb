Canto 9: LiberationChapter 18: King Yayāti Regains His Youth

Bhaktivedanta VedaBase: Śrīmad Bhāgavatam 9.18.51

evam varsha-sahasrāni

manah-shashthair manah-sukham

vidadhāno 'pi nātripyat

sārva-bhaumah kad-indriyaih

SYNONYMS

evam — in this way; varsha-sahasrāni — for one thousand years; manah-shashthaih — by the mind and five knowledge-acquiring senses; manah-sukham — temporary happiness created by the mind; vidadhānah — executing; api — although; na atripyat — could not be satisfied; sārva-bhaumah — although he was the king of the entire world; kat-indriyaih — because of possessing impure senses.

TRANSLATION

Although Mahārāja Yayāti was the king of the entire world and he engaged his mind and five senses in enjoying material possessions for one thousand years, he was unable to be satisfied.

PURPORT

The kad-indriya, or unpurified senses, can be purified if one engages the senses and the mind in Krishna consciousness. Sarvopādhi-vinirmuktam tat-paratvena nirmalam [Cc. Madhya 19.170]. One must be freed from all designations. When one identifies himself with the material world, his senses are impure. But when one achieves spiritual realization and identifies himself as a servant of the Lord, his senses are purified immediately. Engagement of the purified senses in the service of the Lord is called bhakti. Hrishīkena hrishīkeśa-sevanam bhaktir ucyate. One may enjoy the senses for many thousands of years, but unless one purifies the senses, one cannot be happy.

Thus end the Bhaktivedanta purports of the Ninth Canto, Eighteenth Chapter, of the Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam, entitled "King Yayāti Regains His Youth."
